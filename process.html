How I Work

The process
behind every
project.

Every course I deliver follows the same disciplined process — from the first discovery call to post-launch evaluation. No surprises, no scope creep, no "we'll fix it in review." Here's exactly how it works.

Design Philosophy

Five principles that guide
every design decision.

🎯
01
Performance over completion
Every course starts with one question: what do people need to DO differently? Not what do they need to know. Training that changes knowledge but not behaviour is expensive wallpaper.
🏗️
02
Architecture before content
Accessibility, tab order, colour contrast, and interaction design are set in the template before a single piece of content is written. You cannot retrofit quality into a finished course.
✂️
03
Cut ruthlessly
The client always wants more content than learners need. My job is to identify the minimum information required to practise the right decisions — and cut everything else without apology.
🔁
04
Review early, not late
Stakeholder feedback on a 10-slide prototype costs 2 hours to implement. The same feedback on a finished 40-slide course costs 40 hours. I prototype fast and get alignment before building.
📏
05
Measure what matters
Completion rates prove training happened. Behaviour change and business results prove training worked. I design for Level 3 and 4 measurement from the start, not as an afterthought.
🤝
06
Transparent communication
Weekly status updates. Honest scope conversations. Clear documentation of every decision and its rationale. No surprises at the review stage — ever.
Phase by Phase

The five phases
of every project.

Click each phase to see the steps, deliverables, and tools involved.

01

Discover & Define

Before any design decisions are made, I need to understand the actual problem. Not the training request — the underlying performance gap. Most projects start with "we need a course on X" when the real need is "we need people to stop doing Y." Discover phase finds the difference.

This phase uses Action Mapping to establish the measurable business goal, identify the specific on-the-job decisions that drive that goal, and set the success criteria that will tell us if training worked — before development starts.

Deliverables
Needs analysis reportPerformance gap definitionAudience analysisAction mapSuccess criteria documentProject brief
Tools
Stakeholder interviewsSurveysAction MappingLMS data review
1.1
Stakeholder interviews
Structured conversations with the business sponsor, subject matter experts, and ideally 3–5 representative learners. Goal: understand the actual performance problem, not the assumed training solution.
1.2
Audience analysis
Prior knowledge, learning environment, motivations, barriers, technology access, language needs. This determines every design decision from cognitive load to interaction complexity.
1.3
Content & resource audit
Review existing materials — policy documents, current courses, job aids, previous training feedback. Identify what can be reused, what needs updating, and what needs creating from scratch.
1.4
Action mapping
Map the specific on-the-job decisions and behaviours that drive the business goal. This becomes the structural foundation of the entire course — and the primary tool for cutting unnecessary content.
1.5
Project brief sign-off
Document the agreed scope, timeline, technical specifications, review process, and sign-off criteria. Prevents scope creep and misaligned expectations throughout development.
02

Design & Architect

Design phase translates the action map into a detailed course blueprint. Learning objectives are written using Bloom's verbs at the correct cognitive level. Assessment strategy is designed before content — what will learners be asked to DO to prove they've met the objective? The interaction design is specified for each slide before any authoring tool is opened.

For projects with accessibility requirements, the colour palette, tab order convention, and keyboard-operable interaction templates are established here — before any content exists to retrofit them onto.

Deliverables
Learning objectivesAssessment blueprintStoryboard (full script)Interaction design specVisual style guideAccessibility template
Tools
Bloom's TaxonomyGagné's 9 EventsWord / Google DocsStoryline Master Slides
2.1
Learning objective writing
Every objective uses a Bloom's verb at the target cognitive level. "Learners will identify" (L1) is very different from "learners will apply" (L3). The objective determines every downstream design decision.
2.2
Assessment-first design
Before writing a single content slide, I design the assessment. What will learners be asked to demonstrate? This prevents the most common design failure: assessments that test what's easy to test rather than what matters.
2.3
Full script storyboard
Every slide fully scripted — narration word-for-word, on-screen text, visual descriptions, interaction specifications, and branching logic. SME review happens on the storyboard, not the built course.
2.4
Rapid prototype
A 3–5 slide clickable prototype built in the actual authoring tool and shared for stakeholder review. Confirms visual direction, interaction feel, and navigation before full development begins.
2.5
Accessibility architecture
Colour palette tested against WCAG 4.5:1 minimum. Tab order convention documented. Keyboard-operable interaction templates built into the master. This is the last easy chance to get accessibility right.
03

Develop & Build

Development phase builds the course from the approved storyboard. The prototype becomes the Alpha version — all content, all interactions, all media. No decisions are made during development that weren't made in Design phase. If something needs to change, it goes back to the client for sign-off, not silently altered.

Media production — Vyond animation, Blender 3D, Camtasia recordings, custom JavaScript interactions — happens in parallel with slide development. SCORM/xAPI packaging is configured and tested against the target LMS before any review.

Deliverables
Alpha build (.story file)SCORM/xAPI packageMedia assetsLMS test resultsQA checklist
Tools
Storyline 360Rise 360VyondBlender 3DZapWorksCamtasiaJavaScriptSCORM Cloud
3.1
Template & master slide build
All slide masters built before content slides. Fonts, colours, accessible focus indicators, tab order defaults — established once, inherited everywhere. No per-slide accessibility patches.
3.2
Content development
Slide-by-slide build from the approved storyboard. Text, images, interactive elements. Custom JavaScript where the interaction requires capability beyond Storyline's built-in library.
3.3
Media production
Vyond animations, Blender 3D sequences, screen recordings, audio narration, and any AR/VR elements produced and integrated. All video content captioned before embedding.
3.4
SCORM/xAPI packaging
Published to the correct SCORM version for the target LMS. xAPI statements configured if required. Tested in SCORM Cloud and the target LMS before any client review.
3.5
QA & accessibility audit
Full keyboard-only playthrough. Colour contrast check. Screen reader test. Branching logic verification. All items on the pre-publish accessibility checklist confirmed before Alpha delivery.
04

Review & Refine

Review cycles are structured and time-boxed. Each round has a defined focus: Alpha reviews content accuracy; Beta reviews interactions and flow; Gold review confirms final sign-off. Feedback outside the defined scope of a review round is documented for the next round — not silently absorbed into the current build.

Every piece of feedback is acknowledged in writing, with either a confirmation that it has been implemented, a rationale for why it hasn't, or a proposed alternative approach. No feedback disappears.

Deliverables
Alpha review (content)Beta review (interactions)Gold master (final)Change logSign-off documentation
4.1
Alpha review — content accuracy
SME and stakeholder review focused exclusively on content correctness. Is the information accurate? Are the scenarios realistic? Is anything missing that's essential to the objective?
4.2
Alpha revisions
All Alpha feedback implemented, documented in the change log. Any feedback that's out of scope, contradictory to the approved storyboard, or would significantly extend timeline is flagged for discussion.
4.3
Beta review — experience & flow
Full playthrough of the revised build. Focused on: does the course flow logically? Are interactions intuitive? Does navigation work? Are feedback messages clear and instructive?
4.4
Beta revisions & accessibility re-check
Beta feedback implemented. Full accessibility re-check after any structural changes — moving objects can reset tab order. SCORM/xAPI re-tested if any triggers were modified.
4.5
Gold review & sign-off
Final review. Minor corrections only — no new content, no structural changes. Formal written sign-off obtained before LMS publishing. This document protects both parties.
05

Launch & Evaluate

Launch is not the end — it's the beginning of the measurement phase. Kirkpatrick Level 1 (learner reaction) data is designed into the course before publishing. Level 2 (learning) is evidenced by the assessment. Level 3 (behaviour change) requires follow-up observation or performance data — planned before launch, not attempted retroactively.

Post-launch, I'm available for technical support, LMS issues, and the minor adjustments that always emerge once real learners encounter the course. Nothing goes to voicemail.

Deliverables
LMS-ready packageFacilitator guideEvaluation planPost-launch supportSource files
Evaluation Framework
Kirkpatrick L1–L4xAPI analyticsPower BILMS reports
5.1
LMS publishing & configuration
Upload to LMS, configure completion rules, reporting settings, and any adaptive learning parameters. Tested with a real learner account before general release.
5.2
Learner communication
Launch email template provided with course purpose, completion instructions, and expected duration. Clear communication increases completion rates by 20–30% in most deployments.
5.3
Level 1 & 2 evaluation
Post-course feedback survey results reviewed within 2 weeks of launch. Assessment scores analysed for patterns — questions with very high or very low correct rates indicate design issues worth addressing.
5.4
Level 3 follow-up plan
30/60/90-day observation or performance measurement plan. Who will observe what behaviour? What performance metric will indicate transfer? This must be planned before launch or it won't happen.
5.5
Post-launch support & handover
Source files, documentation, and any update procedures handed over. Post-launch technical support window agreed. Minor updates within agreed scope handled without additional charges.
Typical Timeline

What a standard project
actually looks like.

A 30-minute Level 2 (standard complexity) Storyline 360 course with one language and standard LMS delivery. Timeline assumes good SME availability.

Week 1–2
Discover & Define
Stakeholder interviews, audience analysis, content audit, action mapping, project brief sign-off.
Week 3–5
Design & Architect
Storyboard writing, rapid prototype, stakeholder review, revised storyboard sign-off, accessibility template.
Week 6–9
Develop & Build
Full course build, media production, QA and accessibility audit, SCORM packaging, Alpha delivery.
Week 10–11
Review & Refine
Alpha review, revisions, Beta delivery, Beta review, Gold master sign-off.
Week 12
Launch & Evaluate
LMS publishing, learner communication, evaluation plan activation, post-launch support.
Timeline note
Timeline assumes good SME availability (2–3 business day review turnaround) and no major scope changes after storyboard sign-off. Complex projects, localisation, AR/VR, or custom JavaScript development extend timelines proportionally. Use the Effort Estimator for a more accurate estimate for your specific project.
