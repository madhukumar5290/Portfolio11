Laundering

How scenario-based design reduced violations by 34% — and why the original 45-slide course was the wrong solution to the right problem.

The Problem

The client had a course. They had a compliance problem.
These were not the same thing.

A retail banking client approached me with a request to "update their AML training." What they handed over was a 45-slide course that systematically walked employees through the three stages of money laundering, with definitions, regulatory references, and a 10-question multiple choice quiz at the end. Completion rates were 94%. Compliance violations were still occurring.

The violation data told a different story than the completion data. Employees were completing the course and passing the quiz — because the quiz tested whether they'd read the slides, not whether they could make the right decision in a real situation. The actual violations weren't happening because employees didn't know what money laundering was. They were happening because employees were approving high-risk transactions without escalating — either because the urgency pressure felt justified, or because they weren't confident they'd correctly identified the red flags.

The course was answering the wrong question. "What is AML?" was already answered. The question that needed answering was: "When I'm under pressure from a client to approve this transaction, what do I actually do?"

This is the single most common design failure in compliance training. The training addresses awareness. The problem requires application.

The Approach

Action Mapping first. Content second.

The first step was to reframe the brief entirely. Instead of "update the AML course," the project became: "eliminate the specific decision failures that are generating compliance violations." This required a conversation with the compliance team that uncovered the actual violation patterns — not a generic AML overview, but specific scenario types where staff were making wrong calls.

The action mapping process identified three decision types that accounted for 84% of violations:

1. Unusual transaction pattern under client pressure

A longstanding client requests an unusual transaction and implies the relationship is at risk if it's delayed. The employee approves without triggering the escalation protocol — because the social pressure feels more immediate than the regulatory obligation.

2. Incomplete Customer Due Diligence under time pressure

A new account opening with minor documentation gaps. The employee approves with a mental note to "follow up later" — because the queue is long and the gap seems minor. The follow-up never happens.

3. Change of account instruction without verification

A customer contacts the branch to change their registered account number, citing an emergency. The employee processes the change using the new contact details provided by the caller — which is the classic Authorised Push Payment fraud pattern.

Each of these failures isn't a knowledge gap. Every employee who made these errors knew the AML policy. The failures happened under conditions of pressure, ambiguity, and time constraints — exactly the conditions that classroom-style training doesn't prepare people for. The solution had to put learners inside those conditions.
The Design

22 minutes. 3 scenarios. No policy slides.

The redesigned course contained no policy information slides. None. Everything the learner needed to know was embedded in the scenarios themselves — delivered at the point of need, not front-loaded as abstract content they'd forget before ever encountering a real transaction.

Course architecture

The course opened directly with a scenario — no welcome slide, no objectives list, no "by the end of this course you will be able to..." The opening scene placed the learner inside a real conversation with a frustrated client. No context provided. Just the situation.

Each of the three core scenarios followed a deliberate structure:

  • Hook: Immersive scene-setting with character dialogue, establishing the pressure or ambiguity the learner would face
  • Decision point: Three realistic choices — each a decision real employees have made, with genuine rationale behind each wrong answer
  • Consequence branch: Wrong choices led to realistic outcomes — a compliance audit, a client complaint, a reputational incident — not a cartoon "Wrong! Try again"
  • Corrective guidance: After every choice (correct or incorrect), the course explained the regulatory principle at play — information the learner now needed because they'd just encountered the situation that made it relevant
  • Minimum necessary information: Regulatory references, policy text, and supplementary detail available via expandable "Need to know" panels — accessible but not compulsory

JavaScript interactions

Custom JavaScript handled the branching logic — Storyline's built-in branching would have required duplicating slides for each path. The JS implementation used a decision-tree data structure that tracked the learner's choices and accumulated a risk score, which drove the final outcome screen and personalised the debriefing content based on which specific errors (if any) the learner had made.

Accessibility implementation

All three scenarios required keyboard navigation throughout — choice selection, consequence viewing, and information panel expansion. Tab order was explicitly set on every interactive slide. Character dialogue was provided both as audio narration and on-screen text. All images used descriptive alt text and were excluded from tab order. The course was tested with NVDA screen reader before delivery.

The Results

Behaviour change, not completion data.

The compliance team ran a 6-month post-launch analysis comparing violation rates with the same period in the previous year. The three violation types targeted by the course dropped by 34% in aggregate. The first-attempt pass rate on the assessment rose from 61% (previous course) to 89% — and more importantly, the assessment was now testing application (scenario-based decisions) rather than recall (multiple choice definitions).

Learner feedback surveys showed a significant shift in perceived relevance: 87% of learners rated the new course as "directly relevant to my daily work" compared to 34% for the previous version. The most frequent open-text response: "This is the first compliance course I've done that actually showed me a situation I've been in."

The content cut was the most significant design decision in the project. The original brief contained 42 information points the client wanted to include. After action mapping, 8 remained — delivered contextually within scenarios rather than as slides. The other 34 were genuinely unnecessary to support the decisions the course was designed to improve.

The localised versions (Hindi and Arabic) were produced using the same storyboard structure, with the Arabic version requiring full RTL layout adaptation and a separate audio recording session. Both passed regional compliance review on first submission.

What I Would Do Differently

Honest reflection.

The course would have benefited from a fourth scenario focusing on digital transaction channels — the original three scenarios were all branch-based, but a significant proportion of AML risk in this organisation sits in online banking operations. This was identified during the discovery phase but cut for timeline reasons. A module two covering digital channels was scoped and delivered in the following quarter.

The Kirkpatrick Level 3 evaluation (behaviour observation) was self-reported by managers rather than directly observed, which introduces reporting bias. A more rigorous evaluation would have used a standardised observation checklist completed by trained observers — something worth building into the project scope from the start rather than designing retrospectively.
